# Disruption Scenarios — Jewelry Insurance Market

> **Note:** These scenarios are diagnostic tools designed to reveal market vulnerabilities. They are written as vivid narratives from the perspective of potential disruptors, not as literal strategic recommendations. Use them to identify where the market is structurally weak.

---

## Scenario 1: The "Restoration Specialist" Disruption

**Year: 2028. A jewelry restoration startup becomes the #1 insurance provider by embedding specialists instead of underwriters.**

Jessica is a master jeweler in Chicago. For 15 years, she's run a restoration shop—fixing bent prongs, restringing pearls, replacing lost stones. One day, she notices a pattern: customers bring in damaged jewelry and ask, "Will my insurance cover this?" The answer is always complicated. They need to file a claim, send photos, wait 2-3 weeks, get a decision that often denies edge cases because the underwriter doesn't understand jewelry.

Jessica starts "JewelGuard"—an insurance company, but run by restoration specialists, not insurance actuaries.

Here's how it works:
- JewelGuard partners with 500+ independent jewelry restoration shops across the US
- Customer insures jewelry and names their local shop as the "preferred restorer"
- If damage occurs, customer visits their local shop (not a claims processor)
- Specialist assesses damage on-site, takes photos, and authorizes repair within hours (not days)
- JewelGuard pays the specialist directly; customer leaves with fixed jewelry
- Insurance premium: 1.8% (slightly higher than BriteCo) but claims experience is so good that customers stay for 15+ years

By 2028:
- JewelGuard has 40,000 policies and growing 60% YoY
- NPS 92 (vs. Jewelers Mutual's 80, BriteCo's 65)
- Retention rate 88% (vs. industry 70-75%)
- Operating margin 18% (better than expected, because specialists are cheaper than underwriters)

**Why this worked:**
- Claims execution was the InsurTech Achilles heel; JewelGuard fixed it by replacing underwriters with specialists
- Rebuilt trust relationship between insurer and customer (customer knows their specialist)
- Turned claims from a painful process into a convenient service ("I know exactly where to go")
- Network of specialists becomes a defensible moat (hard for incumbents to replicate because it requires rebuilding local relationships)

**What incumbents missed:**
- Jewelers Mutual thought insurance was about underwriting skill; it's about trust
- BriteCo optimized for fast *binding* (60-second quote) but not fast *claims* (still takes weeks)
- Zillion is B2B2C only; missed direct-to-consumer opportunity in restoration market
- None of them thought to make claims a *service*, not a *process*

## Scenario 2: The "Generational Wealth Platform" Disruption

**Year: 2027. A fintech startup bundles jewelry insurance with estate planning and becomes the #1 provider for 55-70 year-olds.**

Marcus is a financial advisor in Boston serving high-net-worth clients ($5M-$50M portfolios). He notices a recurring problem: clients with substantial jewelry collections ($100K-$500K) have no clear plan for inheritance. They have jewelry appraisals scattered across three jewelers, no centralized insurance, and when they die, their heirs fight over valuations and authenticity.

Marcus partners with a fintech startup to build "HeritageCo"—a platform that bundles jewelry insurance with inheritance planning, estate documents, and beneficiary management.

Here's how it works:
- Customer (age 55-75, net worth $2M+) links their jewelry collection to HeritageCo
- Insurance appraisals are automatically generated and attached to will/trust documents
- Beneficiaries are designated in the app; at death, beneficiaries get instant access to:
  - Authenticated appraisals (no disputes about value)
  - Insurance documentation (claims are pre-authorized)
  - Guidance on liquidation (if they want to sell) or keeping (if they want to inherit)
- HeritageCo makes money from insurance premiums (slightly higher: 2%) + estate planning fees ($500-1K per setup) + optional dispute resolution services
- Partnerships with estate attorneys and wealth advisors drive CAC to near-zero (advisors refer customers)

By 2027:
- HeritageCo has 12,000 policies in 55-75 age segment (where competitors haven't focused)
- Average premium per customer: $2,400/year (vs. market average $800/year) because HNW customers have bigger collections
- CAC $0 (advisor partnerships)
- Lifetime value $36,000 (vs. $9,600 for average customer)

**Why this worked:**
- Jewelers Mutual serves HNW but hasn't bundled inheritance planning
- Estate planning is an unmet need that insurance companies aren't thinking about
- Advisors and attorneys are trusted channels; bundling through them gives instant credibility
- High-net-worth customers are willing to pay premium pricing for bundled convenience (no Sherpas on shopping)
- Estate planning creates "stickiness" (customers don't shop around once estate plan is set)

**What incumbents missed:**
- They thought insurance was about loss coverage; it's also about peace of mind + family harmony
- HNW segment is served by Chubb + Jewelers Mutual but not *deeply*; room for specialist
- Estate planning bundling is adjacent vertical; incumbents didn't expand into it
- Distribution through advisors/attorneys is highly efficient for HNW but requires different positioning (not "save money," but "protect legacy")

## Scenario 3: The "Secondhand Luxury Platform" Disruption

**Year: 2026-2027. A luxury resale platform bundles authentication + insurance and becomes #1 distributor of jewelry insurance (by volume).**

Sarah runs TheRealReal's jewelry marketplace (the real company, but imagine an aggressive pivot). She notices that 60% of customers ask, after purchase, "Can I insure this?" Today, the answer is "no, we don't have a partner." This is a missed revenue stream + missed customer stickiness opportunity.

Sarah partners with an insurance startup to embed insurance at checkout:

Here's how it works:
- Customer buys a $5K vintage Cartier bracelet on TheRealReal
- At checkout, they see "Protect your purchase: Insurance $20/year (0.4% of purchase price)"
- They click "yes"; insurance is bound instantly
- Insurance validates authentication using TheRealReal's existing authentication process (no redundant appraisals)
- Claims are handled through TheRealReal + insurer partnership (insurer pays for authenticated replacement/repair through partner network)
- Revenue split: 70% insurer, 30% platform

By end of 2027:
- TheRealReal + insurance partner have written 500,000 policies (all secondhand jewelry)
- Average premium per customer: $30-40/year (lower than new jewelry, but volume is 20x higher)
- Total premium revenue: $18M annually
- CAC: $0 (embedded at checkout)
- Retention: 75% (customers keep insurance because it's so cheap and frictionless)

**Why this worked:**
- Secondhand luxury is the fastest-growing jewelry segment; virtually uninsured (34% penetration on *new* jewelry means maybe 5% on *secondhand*)
- Embedding at checkout is frictionless; customers say "yes" because it's cheap + easy
- Secondhand premium is lower but volume is massive (60M households with mid-market jewelry vs. 2.5M engagement rings)
- Authentication is a solved problem for platforms; no need for redundant appraisals
- Traditional insurers had no distribution to secondhand market; platform has all the customers

**What incumbents missed:**
- They focused on *new* jewelry at retail; missed *secondhand* jewelry at platforms
- They didn't recognize secondhand as a distinct segment with different underwriting, pricing, and distribution
- They had no relationships with resale platforms (Vestiaire Collective, Grailed, Poshmark); only with traditional retailers
- They assumed "insurance is bought at point of *initial* purchase"; missed that secondhand buyers also want coverage

## Cross-Cutting Patterns

**What do these scenarios reveal about structural vulnerabilities?**

1. **Incumbents optimize for one moment in the customer journey, not the whole lifecycle.**
   - BriteCo/Zillion optimize for *enrollment* (60-second quote)
   - But customers care about *claims* (when damage happens), *inheritance* (end-of-life planning), and *ongoing engagement* (lifetime relationship)
   - Specialist disruptors can win by owning a different moment (claims, inheritance, ongoing value)

2. **Retail/distribution assumptions are outdated.**
   - Incumbents think distribution is about jewelers (Jewelers Mutual, Zillion) or DTC website (BriteCo)
   - But distribution is moving to: advisors (HeritageCo), resale platforms (secondhand disruptor), specialists/artisans (restoration specialist)
   - New entrants can own distribution channels incumbents haven't built relationships in

3. **Pricing is the wrong lever; stickiness is the right one.**
   - Incumbent compete on price (BriteCo 0.5-1.5% vs. Jewelers Mutual 1-2%)
   - But price compression has limits; margins suffer
   - Disruptors win by building stickiness through bundling (estate planning, specialist relationships, platform embeddedness)
   - Sticky customers accept higher pricing (HeritageCo charges 2% premium but has 88% retention)

4. **Trust is not inherited; it's rebuilt with each interaction.**
   - Jewelers Mutual has 100+ years of brand trust; yet it's losing share to 10-year-old BriteCo because BriteCo earns trust through UX
   - Disruptors can rebuild trust by fixing the most painful interaction:
     - Claims experience (restoration specialist)
     - Estate planning complexity (HeritageCo)
     - Authentication friction (secondhand platform)

5. **Market segmentation by customer type is the real moat.**
   - HNW customers (55-75) are underserved by insurance companies focused on engagement rings (25-40) and watches (30-60)
   - Secondhand jewelry customers are a "non-customer" segment (don't shop at retail, so not served by traditional insurance)
   - Restoration services are a channel no one has thought of
   - Incumbents are too broad-portfolio to specialize deeply in any segment; disruptors can

**The fundamental vulnerability: Incumbents are serving transaction moments (enrollment, claims) while disruptors are building *relationship ecosystems* (advisory partnerships, community platforms, specialist networks, lifecycle services).** The customer of the future doesn't want an "insurance company"; they want a trusted advisor, a community, a convenient service. Jewelry insurance companies haven't figured this out yet.
